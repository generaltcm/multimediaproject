An Economic Dance

The latest chapter in the Classic City's struggle between national and local business

By Thomas Mills and Anshool Deshmukh

L ocal Athens residents are concerned that the Classic City’s culture and vibe are being slowly ousted in favor of large, big-box retailers, hotel chains and residential apartments.

The closure of several local establishments over the past two years, such as Jackson Street Books, NONA and East West Bistro, coupled with the openings of national stores and student focused apartment complexes, such as Georgia Heights, J. Crew and CVS, which opened in 2016, have lowered the confidence of residents that downtown Athens will remain a local friendly area.

Melissa Tufts, the director of the Owens Library and the Circle Gallery at the University of Georgia College of Environment and Design, feels that the recent changes in downtown Athens will leave a lasting, negative impact.

“The changes will definitely de-personalize the street life,” Tufts said. “I just think people feel less comfortable when they're surrounded by these cavernous buildings that shut out the light and air, it just changes the dynamic of how you experience the street.”

Tufts is not the only resident who shares this sentiment. Many locals feel that the recent commercialization in downtown have brought forth cultural, architectural and aesthetic changes.

However, the question remains: Is the issue as big as residents are making it out to be, or are the changing dynamics making a positive impact to the Classic City?
